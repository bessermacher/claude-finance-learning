1. INTRODUCTION AND SCOPE
================================================================================

IFRS 16 Leases was issued by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB)
in January 2016 and became effective for annual reporting periods beginning on or
after 1 January 2019. It replaced IAS 17 Leases, IFRIC 4 Determining Whether an
Arrangement Contains a Lease, SIC-15 Operating Leases — Incentives, and SIC-27
Evaluating the Substance of Transactions Involving the Legal Form of a Lease.

The core objective of IFRS 16 is to ensure that lessees and lessors provide
relevant information in a manner that faithfully represents lease transactions.
This information gives users of financial statements a basis to assess the effect
that leases have on the financial position, financial performance, and cash flows
of the entity.

IFRS 16 applies to all leases, including leases of right-of-use assets in a
sublease, with the following exceptions:

  - Leases to explore for or use minerals, oil, natural gas, and similar
    non-regenerative resources.
  - Leases of biological assets within the scope of IAS 41 Agriculture held by
    a lessee.
  - Service concession arrangements within the scope of IFRIC 12.
  - Licences of intellectual property granted by a lessor within the scope of
    IFRS 15 Revenue from Contracts with Customers.
  - Rights held by a lessee under licensing agreements for items such as motion
    picture films, video recordings, plays, manuscripts, patents, and copyrights
    within the scope of IAS 38 Intangible Assets.

Two practical expedients are available to lessees:

  (a) Short-term leases: A lessee may elect, by class of underlying asset, not
      to apply the recognition requirements to leases with a lease term of twelve
      months or less. In this case, the lessee recognises the lease payments
      associated with those leases as an expense on a straight-line basis over
      the lease term or another systematic basis.

  (b) Leases of low-value assets: A lessee may elect, on a lease-by-lease basis,
      not to apply the recognition requirements to leases for which the underlying
      asset is of low value. The IASB had in mind assets with a value, when new,
      in the range of USD 5,000 or less (e.g., tablet computers, personal
      computers, small items of office furniture, and telephones). This assessment
      is made in absolute terms regardless of the size, nature, or circumstances
      of the lessee.

2. IDENTIFYING A LEASE
================================================================================

A contract contains a lease if it conveys the right to control the use of an
identified asset for a period of time in exchange for consideration.

The identification of a lease requires assessment of whether:

2.1 There is an identified asset

An asset is typically identified by being explicitly specified in a contract.
However, an asset can also be identified by being implicitly specified at the
time it is made available for use by the customer. A capacity portion of a larger
asset is an identified asset only if it is physically distinct (for example, a
floor of a building).

A customer does not have the right to use an identified asset if the supplier has
a substantive substitution right throughout the period of use. A substitution
right is substantive only if the supplier has the practical ability to substitute
alternative assets throughout the period of use, and the supplier would benefit
economically from exercising its right to substitute the asset.

2.2 The customer has the right to obtain substantially all of the economic
    benefits from use of the asset

A customer has the right to obtain substantially all of the economic benefits
from use of the identified asset if it has the right to those benefits throughout
the period of use within the defined scope of its right to use the asset (e.g.,
within a specified geographical area or a specified time period).

2.3 The customer has the right to direct the use of the asset

A customer has the right to direct the use of an identified asset throughout the
period of use when either:

  (a) The customer has the right to direct how and for what purpose the asset
      is used throughout the period of use; or

  (b) The relevant decisions about how and for what purpose the asset is used are
      predetermined and the customer has the right to operate the asset
      throughout the period of use without the supplier having the right to
      change those operating instructions; or

  (c) The customer designed the asset in a way that predetermines how and for
      what purpose the asset will be used.

3. LESSEE ACCOUNTING MODEL
================================================================================

3.1 Initial Recognition and Measurement

At the commencement date, a lessee shall recognise a right-of-use (ROU) asset
and a lease liability.

Lease Liability — Initial Measurement

The lease liability is measured at the present value of lease payments not paid
at the commencement date. Lease payments included in the measurement are:

  (a) Fixed payments (including in-substance fixed payments), less any lease
      incentives receivable.
  (b) Variable lease payments that depend on an index or a rate (initially
      measured using the index or rate at the commencement date).
  (c) Amounts expected to be payable under residual value guarantees.
  (d) The exercise price of a purchase option if the lessee is reasonably certain
      to exercise that option.
  (e) Payments of penalties for terminating the lease, if the lease term reflects
      the lessee exercising the option to terminate.

The lease payments shall be discounted using the interest rate implicit in the
lease (IRIIL), if that rate can be readily determined. If that rate cannot be
readily determined, the lessee shall use the lessee's incremental borrowing rate
(IBR). The IBR is the rate of interest that a lessee would have to pay to borrow,
over a similar term, with a similar security, the funds necessary to obtain an
asset of a similar value to the right-of-use asset in a similar economic
environment.

Determination of the IBR is one of the most judgement-intensive aspects of IFRS
16 implementation. Entities typically consider:

  - The lessee's credit rating and credit spreads.
  - Risk-free rates in the applicable currency and tenor.
  - Collateralised vs. uncollateralised rates, with an adjustment for the
    collateralised nature of the lease (the asset itself provides implicit
    collateral).
  - Country/currency risk where operations span multiple jurisdictions.
  - A portfolio approach is generally permitted where leases have similar
    characteristics.

Right-of-Use Asset — Initial Measurement

The ROU asset is measured at cost, comprising:

  (a) The initial measurement of the lease liability.
  (b) Lease payments made at or before the commencement date, less any lease
      incentives received.
  (c) Initial direct costs incurred by the lessee.
  (d) An estimate of costs to dismantle and remove the underlying asset or restore
      the site, recognised as a provision under IAS 37.

3.2 Subsequent Measurement

Lease Liability — Subsequent Measurement

After the commencement date, the lease liability is measured by:

  (a) Increasing the carrying amount to reflect interest on the lease liability
      (using the effective interest method at a constant periodic rate of interest
      applied to the remaining balance of the lease liability).
  (b) Reducing the carrying amount to reflect lease payments made.
  (c) Remeasuring the carrying amount to reflect reassessments or lease
      modifications, or to reflect revised in-substance fixed lease payments.

Right-of-Use Asset — Subsequent Measurement

A lessee measures the ROU asset applying a cost model unless it applies one of
the measurement models described below:

  Cost Model: The lessee measures the ROU asset at cost less accumulated
  depreciation and accumulated impairment losses, and adjusted for remeasurements
  of the lease liability.

  Revaluation Model: If the ROU asset relates to a class of property, plant and
  equipment to which the lessee applies the revaluation model in IAS 16, the
  lessee may elect to apply that revaluation model to all ROU assets relating to
  that class.

  Fair Value Model: If the ROU asset meets the definition of investment property
  and the lessee applies the fair value model in IAS 40, the lessee shall apply
  that fair value model.

Depreciation of the ROU asset follows IAS 16 requirements. If the lease
transfers ownership of the underlying asset to the lessee by the end of the lease
term, or the cost of the ROU asset reflects that the lessee will exercise a
purchase option, the lessee shall depreciate the ROU asset from the commencement
date to the end of the useful life of the underlying asset. Otherwise, the lessee
shall depreciate the ROU asset from the commencement date to the earlier of the
end of the useful life of the ROU asset or the end of the lease term.

4. LEASE TERM AND OPTIONS
================================================================================

The lease term is the non-cancellable period for which a lessee has the right to
use an underlying asset, together with both:

  (a) Periods covered by an option to extend the lease if the lessee is
      reasonably certain to exercise that option; and

  (b) Periods covered by an option to terminate the lease if the lessee is
      reasonably certain NOT to exercise that option.

"Reasonably certain" is a high threshold, significantly higher than "more likely
than not." In practice, entities consider:

  - Contractual terms and conditions (e.g., relative to market terms, penalties).
  - Significant leasehold improvements that would be lost upon non-renewal.
  - The importance of the underlying asset to the lessee's operations.
  - Costs associated with terminating the lease (dismantling, relocation).
  - Historical lease renewal patterns and business planning assumptions.

Reassessment of lease term is required upon the occurrence of a significant event
or a significant change in circumstances that is within the control of the lessee
and affects whether the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise an option not
previously included in the determination of the lease term, or not to exercise an
option previously included. Examples of triggering events include: significant
leasehold improvements near the end of the non-cancellable period; subleasing;
restructuring; disposal of an associated business.

When the lease term is reassessed, the lessee shall remeasure the lease liability
using a revised discount rate (the IBR at the date of reassessment, unless a
change in the interest rate implicit in the lease can be readily determined).

5. LEASE MODIFICATIONS
================================================================================

A lease modification is a change in the scope of a lease, or the consideration
for a lease, that was not part of the original terms and conditions of the lease.

5.1 Modification Accounted for as a Separate Lease

A lessee accounts for a lease modification as a separate lease if both:

  (a) The modification increases the scope of the lease by adding the right to
      use one or more underlying assets; and

  (b) The consideration for the lease increases by an amount commensurate with
      the stand-alone price for the increase in scope.

5.2 Modifications Not Accounted for as a Separate Lease

For modifications not treated as a separate lease, at the effective date of the
modification, the lessee:

  (a) Allocates the consideration in the modified contract applying paragraphs
      12–16 of IFRS 16.
  (b) Determines the lease term of the modified lease.
  (c) Remeasures the lease liability by discounting the revised lease payments
      using a revised discount rate.

The accounting for the ROU asset depends on the nature of the modification:

  - If the modification decreases the scope of the lease, the lessee decreases
    the carrying amount of the ROU asset to reflect the partial or full
    termination. Any difference between the adjustment to the lease liability and
    the ROU asset is recognised in profit or loss.
  - For all other modifications, the lessee adjusts the ROU asset by the amount
    of the remeasurement of the lease liability.

6. VARIABLE LEASE PAYMENTS
================================================================================

Variable lease payments are payments made by a lessee to a lessor for the right
to use an underlying asset that vary because of changes in facts or
circumstances occurring after the commencement date (other than the passage of
time).

6.1 Index-linked or Rate-linked Payments

These are included in the measurement of the lease liability (using the index or
rate at commencement date). Subsequent changes in the index or rate result in
remeasurement of the lease liability with a corresponding adjustment to the ROU
asset when a reassessment event occurs or at least at the date of lease
commencement (for future payments).

6.2 Payments Linked to Performance or Usage

Variable payments linked to performance or usage (e.g., a percentage of revenue
generated from a retail location, payments per kilometre for a vehicle) are
excluded from the lease liability. They are recognised in profit or loss in the
period in which the event or condition that triggers those payments occurs. This
represents a significant change from the IAS 17 treatment where such charges
were sometimes included in minimum lease payments.

6.3 In-Substance Fixed Payments

Payments that appear variable but are in-substance unavoidable are treated as
fixed payments. For example, a lease that requires payment of either EUR 10,000
per month or 1% of monthly revenue, whichever is higher, effectively has a
EUR 10,000 per month floor that is in-substance fixed.

7. LESSOR ACCOUNTING
================================================================================

Lessor accounting under IFRS 16 is substantially unchanged from IAS 17. Lessors
continue to classify leases as either finance leases or operating leases.

7.1 Finance Leases

A lease is classified as a finance lease if it transfers substantially all the
risks and rewards incidental to ownership of an underlying asset. Classification
is made at inception of the lease.

Situations that would normally lead to a finance lease classification include:

  (a) The lease transfers ownership of the underlying asset to the lessee by the
      end of the lease term.
  (b) The lessee has the option to purchase the underlying asset at a price that
      is expected to be sufficiently lower than fair value so that it is
      reasonably certain the option will be exercised.
  (c) The lease term is for the major part of the economic life of the underlying
      asset even if title is not transferred.
  (d) At the inception date, the present value of the lease payments amounts to
      at least substantially all of the fair value of the underlying asset.
  (e) The underlying asset is of such a specialised nature that only the lessee
      can use it without major modifications.

The lessor recognises a finance lease receivable at an amount equal to the net
investment in the lease (present value of future lease payments plus any
unguaranteed residual value accruing to the lessor). Interest income is
recognised using the effective interest method over the lease term.

7.2 Operating Leases

A lessor continues to recognise the underlying asset and depreciates it in
accordance with IAS 16 or IAS 38. Lease payments from operating leases are
recognised as income on a straight-line basis or another systematic basis.

8. SALE AND LEASEBACK TRANSACTIONS
================================================================================

A sale and leaseback transaction involves the transfer of an asset by an entity
(the seller-lessee) to another entity (the buyer-lessor) and the leaseback of
that same asset by the seller-lessee.

8.1 Determining Whether a Transfer is a Sale

An entity applies the requirements of IFRS 15 to determine whether the transfer
of an asset is accounted for as a sale of that asset.

If the transfer is a sale:

  - The seller-lessee measures the ROU asset arising from the leaseback at the
    proportion of the previous carrying amount of the asset that relates to the
    right of use retained. Accordingly, the seller-lessee recognises only the
    amount of any gain or loss that relates to the rights transferred to the
    buyer-lessor.
  - The buyer-lessor accounts for the purchase applying applicable standards, and
    for the lease applying the lessor accounting requirements of IFRS 16.

If the transfer is not a sale (i.e., IFRS 15 criteria are not met):

  - The seller-lessee continues to recognise the transferred asset and accounts
    for the cash received as a financial liability under IFRS 9.
  - The buyer-lessor does not recognise the transferred asset and accounts for
    the cash paid as a financial asset under IFRS 9.

9. PRESENTATION AND DISCLOSURE
================================================================================

9.1 Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position)

A lessee shall either present in the statement of financial position, or disclose
in the notes:

  - ROU assets separately from other assets (if not presented separately, the
    lessee shall disclose which line items include ROU assets).
  - Lease liabilities separately from other liabilities (if not presented
    separately, the lessee shall disclose which line items include lease
    liabilities).

ROU assets for investment property shall be presented within investment property.

9.2 Income Statement

A lessee shall present interest expense on the lease liability separately from
the depreciation charge for the ROU asset. Interest expense is presented within
finance costs; depreciation is presented within operating profit. This results
in a front-loaded expense profile compared to IAS 17 operating lease straight-
line treatment — a point of particular significance for covenant compliance and
for entities with large real estate or fleet portfolios.

9.3 Cash Flow Statement

  - Cash payments for the principal portion of lease liabilities: financing
    activities.
  - Cash payments for the interest portion of lease liabilities: either
    operating or financing activities (applying IAS 7 choice, applied
    consistently).
  - Short-term lease payments, low-value asset lease payments, and variable
    lease payments not included in the lease liability: operating activities.

9.4 Disclosure Requirements for Lessees

IFRS 16 requires extensive qualitative and quantitative disclosures:

  Quantitative disclosures:
  - Depreciation charge for ROU assets by class of underlying asset.
  - Interest expense on lease liabilities.
  - Expense relating to short-term leases.
  - Expense relating to leases of low-value assets.
  - Expense relating to variable lease payments.
  - Income from subleasing ROU assets.
  - Total cash outflow for leases.
  - Additions to ROU assets.
  - Carrying amount of ROU assets by class at period end.
  - Maturity analysis of lease liabilities (undiscounted basis, split by year
    for at least the first 5 years, then aggregate for all subsequent years).

  Qualitative disclosures:
  - Nature of the entity's leasing activities.
  - Future cash outflows not reflected in lease liabilities (variable lease
    payments, extension options not included in the lease term, termination
    options, residual value guarantees).
  - Restrictions or covenants imposed by leases.
  - Sale and leaseback transactions.

10. TRANSITION PROVISIONS
================================================================================

Upon initial application of IFRS 16, entities were permitted to choose between:

  (a) Full retrospective application: Restate all comparative periods as if IFRS
      16 had always been applied. The cumulative effect recognised at the earliest
      period presented.

  (b) Modified retrospective application: No restatement of comparative periods.
      The cumulative effect of initially applying IFRS 16 is recognised as an
      adjustment to the opening balance of retained earnings (or other component
      of equity, as appropriate) at the date of initial application.

Under the modified retrospective approach, lessees were permitted to:

  - Measure the ROU asset at an amount equal to the lease liability (adjusted for
    prepaid or accrued lease payments).
  - Measure the ROU asset at its carrying amount as if IFRS 16 had been applied
    since commencement date (using the IBR at transition date).
  - Apply a single discount rate to a portfolio of leases with similar
    characteristics.
  - Rely on the previous assessment of whether a lease is onerous immediately
    before transition instead of performing an impairment review.
  - Exclude initial direct costs from the measurement of the ROU asset at the
    date of initial application.
  - Use hindsight in determining the lease term if the contract contains options
    to extend or terminate.

11. COMMON IMPLEMENTATION CHALLENGES
================================================================================

11.1 Data Collection and Completeness

Many entities underestimated the volume of arrangements in scope. Contracts
containing embedded leases (e.g., outsourcing arrangements, power purchase
agreements, logistics contracts, IT hosting) required detailed assessment. A
structured lease inventory process with a standard questionnaire across business
units proved essential in most large implementations.

11.2 Incremental Borrowing Rate Determination

For groups operating across multiple currencies and jurisdictions, developing a
consistent IBR framework was a significant exercise. Most large groups worked with
treasury or external advisors to build a rate curve by currency, term, and credit
profile. Rates require updating for new leases and for remeasurement events.

11.3 System and Process Changes

Legacy ERP systems (including earlier versions of SAP) were not designed to
handle the continuous remeasurement and rollforward calculations required under
IFRS 16. Dedicated lease accounting modules (SAP RE-FX / REFX, CoStar, LeaseQuery,
Nakisa) were widely implemented. Integration with the consolidation system
(including SAP BPC or SAP Group Reporting) required careful design, particularly
for intercompany lease eliminations and currency retranslation.

11.4 Intercompany Leases

In consolidated financial statements, intercompany leases (e.g., a parent leasing
an office building to a subsidiary) eliminate in full. However, the subsidiary
continues to recognise the ROU asset and lease liability in its standalone
financial statements. This creates a mismatch that must be managed in the
consolidation journals. Where the parent and subsidiary have different functional
currencies, the ROU asset and liability retranslate at different rates over time,
generating currency translation differences that must be correctly attributed.

11.5 Covenant Implications

The capitalisation of operating leases significantly increased reported debt
levels and assets. Net debt covenants, debt/EBITDA ratios, and return on assets
metrics were all affected. Many entities renegotiated credit agreements ahead of
IFRS 16 adoption. EBITDA itself was boosted (because operating lease expense
moved out of EBITDA and into depreciation and interest below the line), while
interest cover ratios tightened.

11.6 Tax Treatment

The tax treatment of IFRS 16 adjustments varies by jurisdiction. In many
countries, tax deductions continue to be based on cash lease payments, creating
deferred tax assets and liabilities. The unwinding of these deferred taxes must
be projected and disclosed. Transfer pricing implications of intercompany leases
also require attention, particularly where the lease terms were established under
IAS 17 economics.

12. KEY DEFINITIONS SUMMARY
================================================================================

Commencement date: The date on which a lessor makes an underlying asset available
for use by a lessee.

Inception date: The earlier of the date of a lease agreement and the date of
commitment by the parties to the principal terms of the lease.

Interest rate implicit in the lease (IRIIL): The rate of interest that causes the
present value of (a) the lease payments and (b) the unguaranteed residual value
to equal the sum of (i) the fair value of the underlying asset and (ii) any
initial direct costs of the lessor.

Incremental borrowing rate (IBR): The rate of interest that a lessee would have
to pay to borrow over a similar term, and with a similar security, the funds
necessary to obtain an asset of a similar value to the right-of-use asset in a
similar economic environment.

Lease incentives: Payments made by a lessor to a lessee associated with a lease,
or the reimbursement or assumption by a lessor of costs of a lessee.

Lease modification: A change in the scope of a lease, or the consideration for a
lease, that was not part of the original terms and conditions of the lease.

Lease term: The non-cancellable period of a lease, together with both: periods
covered by an option to extend the lease if the lessee is reasonably certain to
exercise that option; and periods covered by an option to terminate the lease if
the lessee is reasonably certain not to exercise that option.

Right-of-use asset: An asset that represents a lessee's right to use an
underlying asset for the lease term.

Underlying asset: An asset that is the subject of a lease, for which the right
to use that asset has been provided by a lessor to a lessee.
